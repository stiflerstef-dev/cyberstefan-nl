🔐 What is Ethical Hacking?

  • Think of it as a professional lock‑smith who tries to pick the locks on a building to see if they’re secure, not to steal.
  • The goal is to find weaknesses before bad actors do, so they can be fixed.
  • It follows strict rules and permission – we only test what we’re allowed to.
  • The findings help make the system stronger, just like a safety drill improves emergency response.

🏢 The System We Examined

  • The machine represents a small office network that stores shared files, user information, and remote‑access tools.
  • It mimics a company’s internal server where employees save documents and log in to work remotely.
  • We looked at it because it was advertised as a practice target to learn how defenders can protect similar real‑world systems.
  • No real data was harmed; we used a harmless copy built for training.

🔓 The Weak Spot We Found

  • A shared folder was open to anyone, like leaving the office supply cabinet unlocked so anybody could walk in and take things.
  • Inside that folder was a program that contained a hidden password, similar to a note with the safe combination taped to the inside of a drawer.
  • The program’s “secret” could be read with a free tool, just as anyone could read the note if they opened the drawer.
  • That password let us log in as a regular user, comparable to getting a guest badge that still lets you walk into restricted areas.

🛡️ Lessons for Everyday Security

  • Never store passwords in software or scripts; treat them like house keys that should never be left visible.
  • Limit who can change or view sensitive settings – give people only the access they truly need (principle of least privilege).
  • Regularly check shared folders and remove old files, just like you’d clean out a cluttered desk to avoid losing important items.
  • Use multi‑factor authentication so that even if a password is seen, an extra step stops intruders.

🌐 Why This Matters to You

  • A similar mistake in a real company could expose customer data, leading to loss of trust and possible legal fines.
  • For managers, it shows why investing in security checks and staff training saves money in the long run.
  • For families, it highlights the importance of securing home Wi‑Fi and not sharing passwords in plain text.
  • Overall, spotting and fixing small gaps early prevents big problems later, like fixing a leak before it floods the basement.
